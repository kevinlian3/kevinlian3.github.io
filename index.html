A brand new fossil fuel industry is taking over British Columbia


From the Rocky Mountains to the Alberta border, across an area the size of Nova Scotia in northeastern BC, more than 25 000 fracking wells pockmark the land. With the purpose of extracting natural gas, each well unleashes huge plumes of flames and pours pollution into the atmosphere and soil 24/7.

Mounting evidence shows that the LNG industry poses serious threats to our health, environment, and climate future. To understand what is happening and why BC LNG is a serious health hazard, let us start by learning about the process of fracking.

How does fracking work, exactly?


Hydraulic fracturing, or more commonly known as fracking, is a technique to extract natural gas from hard-to-exploit rock formations deep into the ground. Fracking is not new, but it is becoming a widespread technology in BC for gas production.

As the natural gas reserves can sit more three kilometres underground, the process begins with the drilling of a long vertical well that extends deep into the earth.

Once the well reaches the target gas formation, drilling then turns horizontal and can continue for a kilometre or more.

A "perforating gun" loaded with explosive charges then punctures tiny holes in these horizontal sections.

Now comes the actual fracking part. Afterwards, water, sand and chemicals are injected into the rock at high pressure. The fluids crack open the shale rock, and the sand holds those cracks open. From the shale, natural gas then begins flowing and up out of the well, where it is eventually shipped to consumers via pipeline.

Fracking has become an issue of international attention and debate, and in some cases condemnation.

France, Bulgaria, and Vermont have prohibited the process, and mora­toriums exist in Quebec, New York, and Delaware. Yet while some areas are banning fracking, BC is allowing it to boom.

Today, more than 25,000 fracking wells scar the boreal forest of northeastern British Columbia.

Much of the controversy surrounding fracking lies in the largely unknown health effects. The potential contamination of drinking water with chemicals used in the fracking process is often cited as the main health concern. While additives make up only 2% of the total flu­id volume, this can represent 40 000 litres of additives injected per well.

An analysis of 353 of these chemicals found that more than 75% could have respiratory, gastrointestinal, dermatological, and ocular effects; 40% to 50% could be neuro-, immuno- and nephrotoxic; 37% could be endo­crine disruptors, and 25% could be carcinogenic. particularly given the potential for drinking water contamination with toxic and carcinogenic chemicals.

In order to start a well, you first need 400 tanker trucks full of water at the site.
It takes approximately 1-8 MILLION gallons of water to complete each job.

Finally, about 70% of that water that was used is deemed no long reusable once the job is done.

New technologies permit wellheads to be situated in close proximity to residential areas, and numerous reports of headaches, nausea, asthma exacerbations, depression, fatigue, and reduced cognitive functioning have been anecdotally attributed to noxious fumes and flare gas.
