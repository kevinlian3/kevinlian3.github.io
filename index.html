A brand new fossil fuel industry is taking over British Columbia


From the Rocky Mountains to the Alberta border, across an area the size of Nova Scotia in northeastern BC, more than 20 000 fracking wells pockmark the land. Each with flare stacks that tower above the spruce and aspen forest, unleashing huge plumes of flames and pouring pollution into the atmosphere and soil 24/7.

Mounting evidence shows that the LNG industry poses serious threats to our health, environment, and climate future. To understand exactly what is happening and why this is industry is a serious hazard, let's start by learning about LNG and the process of fracking.

How does fracking work, exactly?


Hydraulic fracturing, or commonly known as fracking, is a technique the LNG is industry to etra gas from shale rocks. As it involves a number of steps, let's walk through a basic operation for natural gas:

The process begins with the drilling of a long vertical or angled well that into the earth. This shale layer can sit more than 5,000 feet underground and drilling can take as long as a month.

As the well nears the rock formation where the natural gas or oil lies, drilling then gradually turns horizontal and extends as far as thousands of feet.

Then a "perforating gun" loaded with explosive charges is lowered to the bottom and punctures tiny holes in the horizontal section of the casting.

Now comes the actual fracking part. Afterwards, water, sand and chemicals are injected into the rock at high pressure. The fluids crack open the shale rock, and the sand holds those cracks open. From the shale, natural gas then begins flowing and up out of the well, where it is eventually shipped to consumers via pipeline.
