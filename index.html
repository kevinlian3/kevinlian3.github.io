What is Natural Gas?


Let us start by clarifying the term "natural gas". Composed mostly of methane, natural gas is anything but natural. In fact, natural gas is also a fossil fuel, formed from the decay of organisms that lived millions of years ago. Like other fossil fuels, many natural gas deposits are found deep underground. Two of North America's largest gas reserves are located in British Columbia.

To access these deposits, hydraulic fracking must be used. Yes, that is right, BC is fracking.

How does fracking work, exactly?


Hydraulic fracturing, or more commonly known as fracking, is a technique to extract natural gas from hard-to-exploit rock formations deep into the ground. Fracking is not new, but it is becoming a widespread technology in BC for gas production.

As the natural gas reserves can sit more three kilometres underground, the process begins with the drilling of a long vertical well that extends deep into the earth.

Once the well reaches the target gas formation, drilling then turns horizontal and can continue for a kilometre or more.

A "perforating gun" loaded with explosive charges then punctures tiny holes in these horizontal sections.

Now comes the actual fracking part. Afterwards, water, sand and chemicals are injected into the rock at high pressure. The fluids crack open the shale rock, and the sand holds those cracks open. From the shale, natural gas then begins flowing and up out of the well, where it is eventually shipped to consumers via pipeline.
